A proxy is a stand-in card you use in place of a real Magic: The Gathering card for playtesting and casual games — same dimensions, same role at the table, just not an official printing. To make one at home, design the card, export a print-ready file sized to standard card dimensions, print it at 100% scale (with "fit to page" turned off), trim it to 2.5 × 3.5 inches, and sleeve it. That's the whole job, and the rest of this guide walks each step in detail.

## What a proxy is and when to use one

A proxy stands in for a card you don't physically own so you can keep playing. The legitimate uses are everyday ones:

- **Playtesting your own designs.** If you're brewing custom cards, a printed proxy lets you actually shuffle the idea into a deck and see how it plays instead of evaluating it on a screen.
- **Trying a deck before you buy it.** Proxying an expensive list lets you test whether you enjoy it before spending real money on the staples.
- **Replacing a damaged card in casual play.** A creased or water-damaged card that's no longer tournament-legal can be proxied so the original retires safely to a binder.

In all three cases the proxy is a tool for play, not a counterfeit. Keep that distinction and you'll stay on the right side of both the rules and your playgroup.

<Callout tone="note" title="Etiquette and legality">
Proxies are fine for casual play and playtesting **with your group's agreement** — always ask first, since not every table wants them. They are **not** allowed in sanctioned or competitive tournament play. And never sell a proxy or pass one off as a real card: that's counterfeiting, not proxying. PipGlyph uses its own original frames and fonts — not Wizards of the Coast's proprietary assets — so cards you make here are clearly fan creations, which is exactly what you want a proxy to be.
</Callout>

- A **print-ready PDF** laid out to standard card dimensions, 2.5 × 3.5 inches (63 × 88 mm).

For home printing, reach for the PDF. It's already sized correctly, so it takes the guesswork out of scaling — the single most common place proxies go wrong.

## Print settings that actually matter

The most important setting is the simplest one: **print at 100%, or "actual size."** Turn OFF "fit to page," "scale to fit," or "shrink to printable area" in your print dialog. Those options resize the page to your printer's margins, which shaves your card down a few millimeters — just enough that it no longer matches a sleeved real card. The PipGlyph PDF is already the right size; your only job is to stop the printer from "helpfully" resizing it.

A quick pre-print checklist:

| Setting | Set it to | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Scale | 100% / Actual size | Produces a true 63 × 88 mm card |
| Fit to page | OFF | Prevents the printer from shrinking the card |
| Paper size | Letter or A4 | Matches the PDF layout |
| Orientation | Portrait | Cards are taller than wide |
| Quality | Best/High for final, Draft for tests | Draft saves ink while you iterate |
| Color | Color (not grayscale) | Keeps mana and frame colors readable |

After the first print, measure the card with a ruler before you commit to a batch. If it isn't exactly 2.5 × 3.5 inches, scaling snuck back in somewhere — fix it and reprint one before running the rest.

## Paper and materials

What you print on decides how the proxy feels and how opaque it is. There's no single right answer; pick based on how much the deck matters and how long it needs to last.

| Material | Cost | Durability | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Plain printer paper | Cheapest | Low (sleeve it) | Quick playtests and iterating fast |
| Cardstock (~200–300 gsm) | Low | Medium–high | Proxies you'll reuse for a while |
| Sticker/label sheet | Medium | High (applied) | Premium feel and full opacity |

Plain paper is the workhorse. It's flimsy on its own, but sleeved in front of a real card it disappears into the deck. **Cardstock** is sturdier and shuffles better unsleeved, though check that your printer accepts the weight you buy. The **sticker** route gives the best result: print the card on a full-sheet label, cut it out, and apply it to the face of a real basic land. That gives you a genuine card's opacity, weight, and back — a proxy that's indistinguishable from the rest of the deck in hand.

## Cutting and sleeving

Once it's printed, you need clean edges. A craft knife and a steel ruler work, but a **guillotine paper trimmer** is far faster and straighter for more than a card or two, and a **corner rounder** punch gives the proxy real rounded corners so it sits flush in a sleeve. Cut just inside the card's border for the tidiest edge.

Sleeving is what makes a paper proxy actually playable. A bare paper card is thin and see-through — opponents can read it from the back, and it won't shuffle with the rest of the deck. The fix: slide your trimmed proxy into a sleeve **in front of a real card or a basic land.** The land behind it supplies weight and opacity so the proxy is the same thickness as every other sleeved card and shuffles identically. Use the same sleeve brand across the deck so the backs match and nothing stands out.

## Color and printer tips

A few small things separate a proxy that looks decent from one that looks washed out:

- **Run a draft first.** Print in draft mode while you're still tweaking — it's faster and saves ink — then switch to best/high quality only for the final copy.
- **Mind your ink.** Inkjets render frame gradients and art more smoothly; laser printers are crisper on text and don't smear if the card gets damp. Either works.
- **Calibrate if colors look off.** If reds come out orange or the frame looks dull, your printer's color profile may need attention — print a test page and adjust saturation or color balance in the print dialog. Because PipGlyph renders exports with the same engine that draws the preview, the file itself is accurate; any color drift is happening at the printer.
- **Let ink dry** before sleeving, especially on glossy or label stock, so nothing smudges.

## FAQ

### Are proxies legal?

For casual and playtest use, proxies are widely accepted as long as your playgroup agrees to them — always ask before bringing them to a table. They are **not** legal in sanctioned or competitive tournament play, where every card must be an authentic printing. Selling proxies or passing them off as real cards is never acceptable and crosses into counterfeiting.

### What size are MTG cards?

Standard Magic: The Gathering cards are 2.5 × 3.5 inches, or 63 × 88 mm. PipGlyph's print-ready PDF is laid out to exactly these dimensions, so as long as you print at 100% scale your proxy will match a real sleeved card and shuffle in without standing out.

### Why does my printed proxy come out too small?

Almost always because "fit to page" or "scale to fit" is enabled in the print dialog. Those options resize the page to your printer's margins and shrink the card. Set scaling to 100% / actual size, turn fit-to-page off, and measure the first print with a ruler before printing the rest.

### Do I have to sleeve paper proxies?

For real play, yes. A bare paper card is thin and partly see-through, so it doesn't shuffle or hide like the rest of the deck. Sleeving the proxy in front of a real card or basic land gives it weight and opacity, making it feel and shuffle exactly like every other card in hand.
