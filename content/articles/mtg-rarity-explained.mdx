Magic: The Gathering has four standard rarities, shown by the color of a card's expansion symbol: common (black), uncommon (silver), rare (gold), and mythic rare (red-orange). Rarity is a signal of a card's complexity and design space — how many words it carries, how unique its effect is, how much "wow" it's allowed — not a promise that it's stronger. Plenty of commons are format staples, and plenty of rares do nothing on their own until you build a deck around them.

## The rarities in order

Every card in a modern Magic set is printed at one of four rarities. The rarity lives in the card's expansion symbol — the little set icon to the right of the type line — and it's encoded by color, so you can read a card's rarity at a glance without flipping it over.

| Rarity | Symbol color | What it signals |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Common | Black | Clean, repeatable, the backbone of Limited |
| Uncommon | Silver | Synergy pieces, archetype signposts, role-players |
| Rare | Gold / yellow | Build-arounds, splashy effects, most legends |
| Mythic rare | Red-orange / bronze | The biggest bombs, marquee legends, chase cards |

There's also a fifth tier you'll see in modern sets: a **special or bonus rarity**, usually drawn in purple. It marks cards that occupy a dedicated special slot in a booster — reprints, serialized cards, or set-specific bonus-sheet cards — rather than the normal common-through-mythic progression. It's a printing-and-collation label more than a design tier, so for card design the four standard rarities are what matter.

## What rarity actually means

Here's the idea that trips up a lot of new designers: **rarity is a complexity budget, not a power level.** A card's rarity tells you roughly how much rules text it's allowed to carry, how novel its effect can be, and how "build-around" it's meant to feel — not how good it is in a vacuum.

Walk up the ladder and the texture changes:

- **Lower rarity = clean and repeatable.** Commons are short, do one legible thing, and show up many times in a Limited pool. They're the format's backbone — the cards you draft a dozen of and that teach the set's mechanics.
- **Higher rarity = more words, more unique effects, more splash.** Rares and mythics get the paragraph-long abilities, the brand-new mechanics, the legends, and the flashy payoffs that make people lean in.

This is why power and rarity come apart so often. Many of the most-played cards in Magic's history are commons — efficient removal, a clean two-drop, a burn spell — because efficiency at low complexity is exactly what a common is for. Meanwhile a rare can be a build-around that's a dead card until your deck is constructed to abuse it. The mythic <Mana cost="{4}{R}{R}" /> dragon that ends the game is rare *because* it's a splashy, deck-defining centerpiece — not because the rules guarantee it beats a common. Designing to rate rather than to rarity is the heart of [designing balanced custom MTG cards](/articles/designing-balanced-custom-mtg-cards).

<Callout tone="tip" title="Rarity is a knob, not a verdict">
When a card feels too strong, raising its rarity doesn't fix the balance — it just hides a busted card behind a rarer symbol. Fix the rate first (cost, stats, text), and let rarity reflect how complex and splashy the card is once it's fair.
</Callout>

## How rarities show up in booster packs

Rarity is also a collation rule — it governs how often each card appears in a booster. The classic math most players remember is roughly one rare, three uncommons, and about ten commons per pack, with a mythic replacing the rare a fraction of the time.

Modern **Play Boosters** changed that arithmetic. Today's packs mix in extra slots — a guaranteed showcase or special card, a wildcard slot whose rarity varies, and a land — so the old "1 / 3 / 10" template no longer describes a pack exactly. Don't anchor on precise per-pack counts; anchor on the *ratio* — many commons, fewer uncommons, scarce rares and mythics. That scarcity is the lever: because a player opens far more commons than mythics, rarity quietly controls how often each effect shows up in a Limited environment, which is exactly what makes it a design tool.

## Rarity as a design tool for a custom set

If you're building a custom set rather than a one-off card, rarity stops being trivia and becomes one of your sharpest tools. Spread across a whole set, rarity is how you pace complexity, teach your mechanics, and decide which effects are abundant versus precious. Use each tier for the job it's built for.

### Commons: teach the set

Commons are your teaching layer. They should be simple, clean, and repeatable, and collectively they should **introduce the set's core mechanics** in their gentlest form. If your set has a keyword, a common or two should wear it plainly so a player meets the mechanic early and often. Commons are also the literal backbone of Limited — they're what people draft in bulk and build their curves from — so keep them honest, at rate, and easy to parse. The single best place to learn this discipline is creature design, where a clean common body is a building block; see [how to design a custom MTG creature](/articles/how-to-design-a-custom-mtg-creature).

### Uncommons: build the archetypes

Uncommons add the connective tissue. This is where **synergy and archetype signposts** live — the gold card that screams "draft this two-color pair," the tribal lord, the role-player that's mediocre alone but shines in the right deck. Uncommons can carry a second ability or one build-around hook, and they're plentiful enough that an archetype actually comes together in a draft. Think of commons as the vocabulary and uncommons as the sentences they let players write.

### Rares and mythics: the payoffs and bombs

Save your **bombs, engines, and complex cards for high rarity.** Rares carry the build-arounds and splashy payoffs; mythics carry the marquee legends and the cards a whole deck is constructed to abuse. This is where text can run to a paragraph and where a creature's stats are allowed to push rate, because the constraint lives in scarcity instead of efficiency. The rule of thumb: if a card needs three paragraphs to explain itself, or it's the centerpiece your deck is named after, it isn't a common. Put your splashiest, most unique, most "wow" designs here — that's literally what the rarity is for.

<Callout tone="note">
A balanced set isn't all bombs. Most of your cards should be commons and uncommons, with rares and mythics as the rare exclamation points. If everything is a build-around, nothing reads as special — and your Limited environment turns to mush.
</Callout>

## FAQ

### What are the MTG rarities in order?

From most to least common: common (black expansion symbol), uncommon (silver), rare (gold or yellow), and mythic rare (red-orange or bronze). Modern sets also use a special or bonus rarity, usually shown in purple, for cards in dedicated special booster slots like reprints or serialized cards.

### Does a higher rarity mean a card is more powerful?

No. Rarity signals complexity and design space — how many words, how unique an effect, how splashy a card is — not a guaranteed power level. Many commons are efficient format staples, and many rares are build-arounds that do nothing until you construct a deck around them.

### What color is each rarity's expansion symbol?

Common is black, uncommon is silver, rare is gold or yellow, and mythic rare is a red-orange or bronze. The expansion symbol is the set icon to the right of the type line, and its color is how you read a card's rarity without any other text.

### How should I assign rarity in a custom set?

Use commons to teach your mechanics with simple, repeatable cards; use uncommons for synergy pieces and archetype signposts; and save bombs, build-arounds, and complex paragraph-long cards for rare and mythic. Keep most of your set at common and uncommon so the rares and mythics still feel special.
