A Magic card's frame is its typography, its border math, its texture — the stagecraft around the art. Players can date a card to within a few years from the frame alone, which makes frame choice one of the most expressive decisions on a custom card: a retro frame says *artifact from the old world*; a sleek M15 frame says *tournament staple*. Here's the lineage, era by era.

## 1993 — the original (Alpha through Mid-1997)

The frame Richard Garfield's team shipped: thick black (or white) borders, a beveled inner edge, color-saturated backgrounds, and the distinctive serif italic flavor text. Text boxes used a parchment-like tint per color; artifacts were brown. Mana symbols sat in a simple top-right run with no drop shadows.

Quirks modern eyes notice: the title sits very close to the top edge, power/toughness floats in a plain box with no art, and the whole card reads *dense*. Custom cards in this frame instantly evoke kitchen-table 1994.

## 1997 — the classic / "retro" frame (Fifth Edition through Scourge)

The first systematic redesign tightened the original rather than replacing it: cleaner bevels, more consistent color identity in the borders, a standardized collector line, and the tan artifact frame everyone remembers. This is the frame of Urza's block and Invasion — for a generation of players, "old Magic" means exactly this look.

Wizards itself now reprints cards in this **retro frame** as a premium treatment, which tells you its nostalgic power. In [PipGlyph's frame picker](/mtg-card-maker) this era is available as the 1997 retro treatment for exactly that reason: customs that want to feel like unearthed history.

## 2003 — the modern frame (Eighth Edition through Magic 2014)

The big one. For Magic's tenth anniversary the frame was rebuilt from scratch: a new typeface (Matrix-derived titles), mana symbols with drop shadows, a pinline-defined art box, grayscale-tinted text boxes, and the power/toughness oval anchored bottom-right. The redesign was controversial for about a year and then became simply *what Magic looks like* for a decade.

Design-wise the 2003 frame is the most neutral canvas: high legibility, restrained color, no holofoil hardware. Custom cards that want "tournament Magic, mid-2000s" pick this.

## 2014 — the M15 frame (Magic 2015 onward)

The current baseline. Subtler than 2003's jump but full of functional changes: the **holofoil security stamp** at the bottom center of rares and mythics, a new font for collector info, a slightly larger art box, and the Beleren typeface for card names — the bold, humanist face PipGlyph also uses for card titles, because it IS the face players associate with card names now.

The M15 era also normalized frame *variations*: the legendary crown arrived in 2017 as an extra frame layer for legendary permanents, and transform/double-faced hardware matured here.

## The showcase era — 2019 to now

Starting with Throne of Eldraine, every set ships **showcase frames**: per-set art-deco treatments, storybook borders, extended art, borderless planeswalkers, retro reprises. The "frame" stopped being one thing and became a wardrobe. What this means for custom designers is permission: alternate treatments are now canon, so a custom set with its own signature frame treatment is playing the same game Wizards plays.

## Choosing an era for your custom card

Three questions pick the frame:

1. **What table is it for?** Playtest proxies for a modern deck want M15-era frames — they'll sit in sleeves next to real modern cards. A gift card for a player who started in 1996 wants the 1997 retro frame.
2. **What does the card's story say?** Frames carry fiction. An ancient relic reads better in an older frame; a sleek tournament rare wants the modern treatment. Mixing eras *within a set* can even be deliberate — a "rediscovered artifacts" subtheme in retro frames inside an otherwise modern set.
3. **How much text do you have?** Older frames have smaller text boxes. A four-line design with reminder text fits the M15 box; the same text in a 1993 frame will shrink. The [live preview](/preview) shows the fit instantly.

## FAQ

### Which frame era is "correct" for proxies?

Match the deck. Proxies for playtesting a Modern or Commander deck should use the M15-era frame so the card reads at a glance like its real neighbors. House-cube and casual proxies are where the older frames shine.

### Can I mix frame eras in one custom set?

Yes, and real sets do it deliberately (retro-frame artifacts in The Brothers' War). Give the era split a *meaning* — old frames for old-world cards — and it reads as intent rather than inconsistency. PipGlyph set pages show all your cards together, which makes era cohesion easy to audit.
